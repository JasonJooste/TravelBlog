Hi BoBers,

With the threat of kidnapping unavoidably in mind, the trip from Esie to Abuja did not start auspiciously. A soldier chased me down on the back of a moto-taxi and interrogated me - not to protective, but suspicious. What was I doing there? Where was I going? After we established I wasn't a spy, he let me go, but told me the area was dangerous. A few kilometres later, a packed sedan overtook me and swung across the road. This time, the "community police" - a group of men keeping the community safe. Again, questions and suspicion. Eventually they calmed down and let me pass with smiles and waves.

Later that day I passed a church ringed with spiked walls and barbed wire - I took a photo, and a man screamed at me that I had no right. I calmed him down and moved on. A little later, I stopped for a quick rest in an open-air church, essentially a gazebo with some chairs in it, and sat doing my Duolingo. Five minutes later, more community police, this time more aggressive. They demanded to see my ID. I refused. They told me to wait for the police. I said I wanted to leave and started wheeling my bike away. One of them grabbed it. It turned into a shouting match, until their boss arrived - not an official, but I showed him my passport anyway to get out of there. He sent me up the road to the military commanding officer, who didn't give a shit and let me go.

I've travelled quite a bit and never experienced this before. People are often scared *for* me, never *of* me. Usually when people tell me the surrounding area is dangerous, they're either being protective of the lily-white soft-soled visitor or are a bit biased about their neighbours. This felt entirely different and left me on edge. A couple of times people ran away when I came too close. The fear was palpable. Luckily for me, the kidnappings and violence had peaked in October 2025, after which the military was sent in. There was certainly a massive military presence - AK-47s, armoured vehicles, checkpoints every few kilometres from various branches of the military and police. On their guard, but polite. Most refused photos but some absolutely insisted.

With heightened senses, I made sure to pass through Lokoja - previously the centre of kidnappings in the area - in the middle of the day. Crossing the Niger river was a huge relief. Soon after, I reached the highly-secured Federal Capital Territory around Abuja and was finally safe. I was tired. Not only of fear, but of the suspicion that came with it. A couple of times I deliberately stopped because I thought I was being followed, mistaking curious motorbikers as spotters for the kidnappers. I don't regret being cautious, but it was exhausting.

Abuja was both a huge contrast to what I'd experienced and a contrast in itself. Like many other purpose-built capitals, it feels like the embodiment of a retrofuturistic 1950's dream-turned-nightmare. The centrepiece of the modest city centre is three massive highways lined with tessellated "luxury" residential estates, with some suburbs for the poor tucked in between. It's not quite the hyper-luxury flex-fest of Lagos. Slightly more old money in the mix.

I didn't ask, but my first contact in Abuja gave off exactly that vibe. She owns a successful business giving music lessons to the city's wealthy and, in a familiar pattern, was very kind and generous to me. She didn't have space to host me, so I stayed on the floor of her employee's tiny tin-and-wood house. I was given free meals at the café and a driver and air-conditioned Lexus for a day of visa hunting - another shocking contrast.

A devout Christian, my host took me to choir practice that evening, where the choir leader openly ridiculed two girls for their lack of enthusiasm. When I casually praised his singing the next day, the school's music director mocked my taste. I decided to find my own place after that and other overreaches. Rich Nigerians can be horribly dismissive of the poor. I'm glad I moved on, because near the hotel I met HEARTMAN, a talented beat maker and rapper. I got to sit and listen to some of his tracks with him. He said he'd recorded most of it on his phone. Incredible.

My main task in Abuja was to get my visa for Equatorial Guinea - a notoriously secretive and closed country whose consulate here is one of the few places that actually grants visas to travellers. Unfortunately, I arrived on a Thursday evening, confirmed the required documents on Friday, and was left with Monday for the application. My 30-day Nigerian visa ticking down with a solid 700km still ahead, I was itchy to leave. But consulates set their own cadence and the ambassador of Equatorial Guinea was especially busy this week.

By Friday I'd packed my things and was waiting outside the embassy with the guards, hoping to get my visa and hit the road immediately, which irritated the embassy staff. While waiting, a shifty guy in wraparound mirrored sunglasses came over and knowingly asked if I was from the CIA. After I refused his request for my phone number and he hassled me further, the guards told him to piss off - after which he stalked the streets a bit before calling the police, who arrived demanding my passport. They went into the consulate to confirm my story, likely irritating the staff further. After the police left apologetically, I was invited inside.

The ambassador's secretary - who seemed to be largely running the show - filled out the visa. She said she wanted to go home and would let the ambassador know later. I paid directly into her account and she made it fairly clear she'd like an extra gift. When I rushed downstairs and brought her seashells from Côte d'Ivoire, she was visibly disappointed. But the visa was done, she wanted to go home, and she waved me out with a wry smile. Still no bribes given after over a year in Africa!

In fact, I didn't leave straight away, and spent one more night in Abuja thanks to a chance message from another cyclist. But that will have to wait for the next entry.

Overcapitalised,

J&BoB
